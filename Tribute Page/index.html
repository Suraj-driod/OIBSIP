01 — Her Story

A Pioneer Against All Odds

Born Maria Sklodowska in Warsaw, Poland, Marie Curie faced obstacles that would have deterred most. Women were barred from higher education in Russian-controlled Poland. Did that stop her? She attended underground "floating universities" and eventually made her way to Paris with little more than determination and an insatiable hunger for knowledge.

In a cramped, leaky shed that served as her laboratory, she discovered not one but two new elements: polonium (named after her homeland) and radium. She coined the very term "radioactivity." She became the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, and remains the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences.

02 — Legacy

What I Admire Most

I

Relentless Curiosity

She spent four years manually processing tons of pitchblende ore to isolate a single gram of radium. That level of dedication to understanding the unknown is almost incomprehensible.

II

Breaking Barriers

In an era when women were expected to stay home, she became a professor at the Sorbonne, the first woman to hold such a position. She didn't ask for permission—she simply excelled.

III

Selfless Service

During World War I, she equipped ambulances with portable X-ray machines and drove them to the front lines herself. She trained 150 women as X-ray technicians. Science, for her, was always about helping humanity.

03 — Milestones

A Life of Firsts

1891

Moves to Paris to study at the Sorbonne

1898

Discovers polonium and radium

1903

First woman to win a Nobel Prize (Physics)

1906

First female professor at the Sorbonne

1911

Wins second Nobel Prize (Chemistry)

1914

Creates mobile X-ray units for WWI

Marie Curie's notebooks are still so radioactive that they must be kept in lead-lined boxes. Anyone who wishes to view them must wear protective clothing. She literally gave her life to science—dying of aplastic anemia caused by radiation exposure.

Her legacy isn't just in her discoveries. It's in proving that passion, persistence, and an uncompromising commitment to truth can overcome any barrier society erects.
